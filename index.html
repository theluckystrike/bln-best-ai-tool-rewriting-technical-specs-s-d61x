Three AI Tools That Actually Make Technical Specs Readable for Stakeholders

Originally published at belikenative.com/best-ai-tool-rewriting-technical-specs-stakeholders

I've been there. You spend hours crafting a technical specification that's precise, detailed, and absolutely correct. Then you send it to the VP of Product, and they reply with "Can you give me the short version?"

The problem isn't your writing. It's the audience. Technical specs are written for engineers, but stakeholders need the "so what" — the business impact, the timeline, the risks. Translating between these two worlds is exhausting, and honestly, it's not the best use of your time.

That's where AI rewriting tools come in. But not all of them handle technical content well. Some dumb things down too much. Others keep the jargon but just rearrange sentences. After testing a bunch of options, I found three tools that actually work for this specific task. Each one fits a different scenario, so you can pick based on what matters most to you — accuracy, tone, or scale.

What Makes a Good Rewriter for Technical Specs?

Before we dive into the tools, let's be clear about what we're looking for. A rewriter for technical specs needs to:

  • **Preserve technical accuracy** — no making up facts or changing specifications
  • **Understand context** — know that "API endpoint" isn't interchangeable with "server"
  • **Adjust reading level** — drop from college-graduate to high-school level without losing meaning
  • **Keep the core structure** — stakeholders still need to know what's being built and why

Most general-purpose AI writers fail on at least one of these. The tools I'm about to share handle all four, but they approach the problem differently.

Tool 1: BeLikeNative — Best for Accuracy and Tone Control

When I need to rewrite a spec for a skeptical stakeholder — someone who's been burned by vague promises before — I turn to BeLikeNative. It's built specifically for this kind of work: taking complex text and making it sound like a human wrote it while keeping every technical detail intact.

Here's how I use it: I paste a paragraph from a spec, select "professional but clear" as the tone, and let it work. The output keeps the technical terms that matter (like "latency" or "throughput") but drops the unnecessary modifiers and passive constructions. For example, a sentence like "The system's asynchronous processing pipeline shall be configured to prioritize low-latency data transmission" becomes "The system will prioritize sending data quickly by handling tasks in the background." Same meaning, much clearer.

The best part? You can tweak the tone for different stakeholders. Executives get the summary version. Project managers get a bit more detail. QA leads get the full picture with clearer language. One tool handles all of them.

I've also used its text simplifier for the most jargon-heavy specs. It's great for those "just tell me what this means in plain English" requests. You paste in the dense stuff, and it strips away the complexity without dumbing down the actual content.

Tool 2: ChatGPT — Best for Custom Workflows

ChatGPT is the Swiss Army knife of AI writing tools. It's not purpose-built for rewriting technical specs, but with the right prompt, it does a solid job. The advantage here is flexibility — you can ask it to rewrite something in a specific format, like a bulleted list or a one-page summary.

The downside? You have to be careful with accuracy. ChatGPT sometimes "hallucinates" details that weren't in the original text. I've seen it add requirements that didn't exist or simplify things to the point of being misleading. So you need to review every output carefully.

Where ChatGPT shines is when you need a specific structure. For example, I'll prompt it with: "Rewrite this technical spec for a product manager. Use plain language. Keep all deadlines and dependencies. Output as a table with columns for feature, timeline, and risk level." That kind of custom formatting is harder to get from purpose-built tools.

But honestly, for day-to-day rewriting where accuracy is critical, I stick with a dedicated tool. The less time I spend fact-checking, the better.

Tool 3: Notion AI — Best for Teams and Scale

If you're working in a team that already uses Notion for documentation, Notion AI is a no-brainer. It lives inside your existing docs, so you can highlight a section and say "simplify this for stakeholders" without leaving the page.

The key strength here is scale. You can rewrite dozens of spec sections in minutes, and they all stay inside your shared workspace. No copy-pasting between tools. No version control issues. Everyone sees the same updated version.

Notion AI is decent at preserving technical accuracy, though it's not as precise as BeLikeNative. It tends to keep more of the original structure, which can be good or bad depending on how clear the original was. For dense specs that need heavy rewriting, I usually run them through BeLikeNative first, then paste the result into Notion for team review.

How to Choose the Right Tool

Here's my simple framework:

  • **If accuracy is non-negotiable** (you're writing specs for safety-critical systems or regulatory compliance): Use BeLikeNative. It preserves the technical details while making the language accessible.
  • **If you need custom formatting or unusual outputs** (like a one-pager, a slide deck summary, or an email): Use ChatGPT with a detailed prompt. Just budget time for review.
  • **If you're rewriting at scale across a large team** (dozens of specs per week): Use Notion AI. The convenience of staying in your workflow outweighs minor accuracy trade-offs.

A Practical Workflow

Here's what I actually do when a stakeholder asks for a "simpler version" of a 20-page spec:

1. Copy the executive summary and key technical sections 2. Run them through BeLikeNative's text simplifier to get a clean, readable version 3. Review for accuracy — always check numbers, dates, and dependencies 4. Add context that the AI couldn't know (like organizational politics or recent changes) 5. Send the result to the stakeholder with a note: "Here's the plain English version. The original spec has all the technical details if you need them."

This takes about 15 minutes for a long spec, compared to an hour or more of manual rewriting. And the output is usually better — more consistent, clearer, and less prone to my own biases about what's "important."

FAQ

**Will these tools change my technical specifications?** They shouldn't if you use them correctly. The good tools (like BeLikeNative) preserve the original meaning and only change the language. But you should always review the output, especially for numbers, dates, and technical constraints. Think of the AI as a translator, not an editor.

**Can I use these tools for regulatory or compliance docs?** Carefully. The tools can simplify the language, but they might miss nuances that matter for compliance. I'd recommend rewriting a section, then having a subject matter expert review it. Never use AI on compliance docs without human oversight.

**Do I need to credit the AI tool when sharing rewritten specs?** Not usually. The AI is a tool, like a spell checker. You're still the author. Just make sure you've reviewed and approved the output before sharing it with stakeholders. If someone asks how you cleaned up the language, feel free to share your process — it's a good conversation starter about improving communication.
